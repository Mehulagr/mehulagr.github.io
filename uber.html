    Rating System 2.0
    Re-designing Uber's rating system for a thoughtful feedback

    • Sept'15

    • Group of 4

    • Guide: Prof. Marty Siegel

    Uber offering service to millions of riders per day has a follows a 5 star rating system. The major problem is its positive bias of rating system and its un-ability to capture qualitative problems. We followed user-centered methodology to design a solution that aimed at providing specific (more qualitative) drivers without increasing interactions for the riders.

    My Role: Literature Study, User Study, Problem Identification, Persona, Ideation and Usability Testing.

    Research

    Uber already offers ride to a million users per day. Uber wants to achieve the higher goal of replacing private car ownership for which they need to create a massive peer-to-peer that people can trust. This trust comes down to rooting out of problematic riders and drivers. Additionally, they have to ensure that the driver provide a premium car-fleet like service to its riders. A lot of this happens through its rating system which has flaws such as bias and subjectivity.

    Primary and Secondary Research were conducted to understand the existing rating system, its problems, its alternatives, users problem with that etc. The common problem was consistent in primary and secondary research. Statement by one of the driver reflects the depth of the problem-

    It’s soul crushing when my rating takes a dive. I spend my first waking moments wracking my brain trying to figure out what could have gone wrong with my rides the night before. - Uber's driver blog

    Problem

    The main problem that we identified with the existing rating system in Uber is that its ambiguous and inaccurate. The system is ambiguous because passengers don't have a common mental model regards to the ratings. One rider gives 3 stars on average, other 4 and whereas other gives 5, for the same experience. This further leads to inaccurracy.

    Moreover, drivers don’t get reasons behind the bad ratings. Providing them reason behind bad rating gives them a fair chance to improve themselves which will prevent uber from deactivating good drivers.

    Problem Statement
    To provide specific feedback to drivers while keeping minimum interaction for the riders.


    Persona

    Two primary users for our designs were passengers and the drivers.

    Astrid (21) | Driver

    Astrid is a dancer. Her English communication is not so good. She has a boyfriend who is teaching her English. She immigrated from France a year ago to become a dancer.

    The dancing jobs that she gets are uncertain. She drives Uber, as an income source when she is between two dancing jobs. Also, her dancing schedule changes a lot. So, she enjoys flexibility of Uber and drives whenever she has time.

    Aaron (21) | Passenger

    Aaron is studying mathematics in finance in New York University. He has a part-time job at Starbucks. He shares his house with his fellow classmate Aman.

    He works 16 hours a week at Starbucks and simultaneously attends classes. In weekdays, he is generally found running between his starbucks job and classes. He is an athlete and he usually goes to sports center 6pm - 7pm. After 8pm, he gets busy with his coursework.

    Final Design

    The experiment was within-subject design with 10 participants using both the systems. The users evaluated both the systems on SUS scale and Attrakdiff questionnaire. They were asked to give out their preference for each system. The SUS score for Visual Feedback system is 72 whereas SUS score for Shape Changing System is 66.5.


    Conclusion

    Tangible interfaces couple the physical world with the digital information. Thus, change in material properties become a natural choice of interaction for such interfaces. Shape changing feedback system was found to be attracting attention of the user by the virtue of its movement and so they have potential to be more effective in catch immediate attraction of user particularly over the distance. The results also suggest that shape change as a feedback fared better to convey system status and ordinal information whereas non-screen visual (illumination based) fared better for quantitative information.
